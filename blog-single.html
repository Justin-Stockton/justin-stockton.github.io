What Building Software for Electrical Contractors Taught Me About Product Design

Reflections from my work as CTO and Founding Engineer at Safe Right Fast Tech, Inc. on why the best product decisions usually start with operational reality, not feature brainstorming.

One of the most useful shifts in my career happened when software stopped being an abstract engineering challenge and became a tool for teams trying to do difficult work under real constraints. Working with electrical contractors changed how I think about product design because the margin for unnecessary friction is small.

In a lot of software environments, people can tolerate a clumsy workflow for a while. They may grumble, but they will still get through it. In contractor operations, awkward software gets exposed quickly. If a workflow adds extra steps, hides critical information, or breaks the natural rhythm of the work, people feel it immediately because it slows down coordination, reporting, handoffs, and accountability.

The field is a product requirement

That sounds obvious, but it changes the way you make decisions. It means the interface is not the product by itself. The surrounding conditions matter just as much: who is entering information, when they are entering it, what they are juggling at the time, and who depends on that information next.

When we build quality assurance apps, opportunity tracking tools, or prefabrication workflows, we are not just creating screens. We are shaping how operational information moves through a business. Good product design has to respect the cadence of how teams already communicate and execute.

Client conversations are part of design

This is one reason I value client-facing meetings so much. Product clarity often shows up long before a ticket is written. A client explains a frustration, a workaround, or a reporting gap, and suddenly the real problem becomes visible. Sometimes what looked like a missing feature is actually a sequencing issue. Sometimes a request for customization is really a sign that the workflow does not match the business process.

Those conversations improve product design because they reveal the pressure points behind the request. If you skip that step and go straight into implementation, you risk building something technically correct but operationally weak.

The strongest product decisions usually come from understanding where work gets stuck, not from guessing what sounds useful in a planning meeting.

Software has to earn trust

In contractor environments, trust matters. Teams need to believe the tool will help them do the job, not create more overhead. That means clarity matters. Speed matters. Error prevention matters. It also means the product should help people feel more confident in the state of the work rather than less certain.

I have found that this pushes us toward simpler flows, sharper labeling, better defaults, and more thoughtful reporting. It also pushes engineering and product closer together. A technically elegant system is not enough if the user experience creates hesitation at the point of use.

What I keep in mind now

The lesson I keep coming back to is that product design improves when it stays close to the operating environment. Listen carefully. Watch for where the handoffs break. Pay attention to what people are forced to remember manually. Build around the actual motion of the work.

That approach has made me a better engineer and a better product partner. It has also made the work more satisfying, because the software is connected to outcomes people genuinely care about.

Built for clarity, speed, and a better first impression.
