Staying Hands-On as a CTO

Technical proximity still matters. The challenge is using it to create leverage instead of noise.

One of the more interesting parts of engineering leadership is figuring out how to stay technically engaged without slipping into unhealthy patterns. As CTO and Founding Engineer at Safe Right Fast Tech, Inc., staying hands-on still matters, but the reason has changed. Early in my career, writing code was mostly about contribution. In leadership, it is more about context, judgment, and leverage.

The danger is obvious. If a manager inserts themselves everywhere, they become a bottleneck, blur ownership, and make it harder for other engineers to grow. But the opposite extreme is not great either. If you drift too far from the code and the systems, you lose touch with implementation cost, architecture pressure, and the real friction the team is dealing with.

Hands-on does not mean everywhere

The most useful technical involvement is selective. I try to contribute where it improves clarity, reduces risk, or helps the team move through ambiguity faster. That might mean shaping an approach early, helping break down a tricky problem, or jumping into implementation in places where context is especially important.

What I try to avoid is casual interference. Engineers do not need a manager proving they can still write code. They need a manager who can support good decisions, spot hidden risk, and step in where their perspective is genuinely useful.

Technical context improves management decisions

Staying close to the work also improves non-coding decisions. Estimation gets better. Tradeoff conversations get sharper. Product discussions become more grounded. It becomes easier to tell the difference between a true engineering risk and a problem that just needs a little more iteration.

That matters when you are balancing delivery, team health, client expectations, and product quality all at once. Good management decisions are easier when they are informed by reality instead of guesswork.

The goal is not to stay hands-on for identity. The goal is to stay close enough to the work to make better decisions and help the team succeed.

Ownership still belongs to the team

The most important guardrail is ownership. Even when I am contributing directly, I want the team structure to remain healthy. Engineers should still own their areas. Decisions should still be legible. My involvement should make work clearer, not more dependent on me.

That usually means being explicit about where I am contributing and why. It also means knowing when to step back. Some of the best management work happens when you help create the conditions for good execution and then let strong engineers run.

The balance is worth learning

I do not think there is one perfect formula for every team. The right balance depends on team size, seniority, product complexity, and business pressure. But I do think the question is worth asking often: where does my technical involvement create leverage, and where does it create drag?

That question has been useful for me, especially in environments where I am leading people, designing systems, meeting with clients, and still contributing to delivery. Staying hands-on can be a strength, but only when it is paired with discipline.
