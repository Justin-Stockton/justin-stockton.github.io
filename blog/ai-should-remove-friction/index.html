AI Should Remove Friction, Not Add Noise

The most valuable AI use cases are usually the quiet ones that improve clarity, reduce repetition, and help teams move faster with less drag.

AI is becoming part of almost every technical conversation, but I think a lot of the framing is still too loud. The useful question is not whether AI is impressive. The useful question is whether it removes meaningful friction from real work.

As CTO at Safe Right Fast Tech, Inc., I care less about novelty and more about leverage. If a tool helps engineers draft faster, compare options more clearly, summarize context, or automate repetitive operations, that matters. If it mostly creates extra output to sort through, then it is probably increasing noise instead of reducing it.

Good AI use cases are usually boring in a good way

The strongest implementations I have seen are not theatrical. They help with the kinds of tasks that drain attention: repetitive documentation, low-value transformations, initial drafts, context gathering, or analysis that benefits from a fast first pass. These are not glamorous wins, but they compound.

That matters because engineers do not need more stimulation. They need more room for judgment. The more low-value churn you can remove, the more energy remains for architecture, debugging, product tradeoffs, and customer problems.

AI should fit the workflow, not dominate it

One pattern I am skeptical of is when teams reshape their process around a tool before they understand the actual value it provides. That usually produces awkward adoption. People feel pushed into new behavior that does not map cleanly to the work.

A better approach is to start with the friction. Where are people wasting time? Where is context getting lost? Where are quality checks repetitive but still important? If AI can help there, adoption becomes more natural because the benefit is visible immediately.

The best AI tools do not demand attention. They return attention.

Human judgment is still the multiplier

I do not think AI reduces the value of experienced engineers or product leaders. If anything, it raises the value of judgment. When output becomes easier to generate, the ability to evaluate quality, detect risk, and choose direction matters even more.

That is why I think the real AI opportunity is not replacing thinking. It is clearing enough noise that people can spend more time on the parts of the work that still require strong thinking.

What I am watching for

The most interesting AI tools to me are the ones that make teams more coherent: better context transfer, better drafting, better operational visibility, better repetitive task reduction. If a tool can do that without introducing fragility or confusion, it is probably worth deeper attention.
