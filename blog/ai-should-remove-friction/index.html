AI Should Remove Friction, Not Add Noise

The most valuable AI use cases are usually the quiet ones that improve clarity, reduce repetition, and help teams move faster with less drag.

AI is becoming part of almost every technical conversation, but much of the framing is still too loud. The useful question isn't whether AI is impressive; it's whether it removes meaningful friction from real work.

As CTO at Safe Right Fast Tech, Inc., I care less about novelty and more about leverage. If a tool helps engineers draft faster, compare options more clearly, or automate repetitive operations, that matters. If it mostly creates extra output to sort through, it's likely increasing noise instead of reducing it.

Good AI use cases are usually boring... in a good way

The strongest implementations aren't theatrical. They assist with the tasks that drain attention: repetitive documentation, low-value transformations, or initial context gathering. These aren't glamorous wins, but they compound.

Engineers don't need more cognitive load; they need more room for judgment. The more low-value churn you can remove, the more energy remains for architecture, debugging, and solving customer problems.

AI should fit the workflow, not dominate it

I'm skeptical of teams that reshape their entire process around a tool before they understand its actual value. That usually leads to awkward adoption. People feel pushed into behaviors that don't map cleanly to the work.

A better approach is to start with the friction. Where is context getting lost? Where are quality checks repetitive? If AI can help there, adoption happens naturally because the benefit is visible immediately. The best AI tools don't demand attention; they return it.

Human judgment is the multiplier

AI doesn't reduce the value of experienced engineers or product leaders. If anything, it raises the value of judgment. When output becomes easier to generate, the ability to evaluate quality and detect risk matters even more.

The real opportunity isn't replacing thinking. It's clearing enough noise so that people can spend their time on the parts of the work that require deep thinking.

What I'm watching for

I'm interested in the tools that make teams more coherent: better context transfer, operational visibility, and repetitive task reduction. If a tool can do that without introducing fragility, it's worth the investment.
