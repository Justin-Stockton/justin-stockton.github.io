How I Form First Impressions of New Technology

My first reaction to a new tool is less about excitement and more about fit, cost, and whether the upside is strong enough to deserve real attention.

I like new technology, but I try not to confuse curiosity with commitment. A lot of tools are easy to admire in isolation. The harder question is whether they become more useful or less useful once they enter a real team environment.

Over time I have started using a more grounded first-impression framework. It helps me decide whether something deserves deeper evaluation or whether it is just interesting from a distance.

First: what pain does it remove?

If I cannot quickly identify the friction a tool reduces, my interest drops fast. New technology does not have to solve a massive problem, but it should solve something concrete. Better speed, better clarity, lower operational burden, fewer repetitive tasks, stronger visibility, or a cleaner developer experience are all legitimate answers.

Second: what is the adoption tax?

Every tool asks for attention. Some ask for a little. Some ask for a lot.... Setup, training, workflow changes, integration complexity, maintenance, and ecosystem maturity all count. A tool with moderate upside and a high adoption tax is often less attractive than it first appears.

This matters even more in leadership roles. Team attention is limited. If you spend it badly, you do not just lose time. You lose momentum.

A good first impression is not “this is cool.” It is “I understand where this fits, why it matters, and what it would cost us to care.”

Third: does it match the team and product reality?

Context matters more than abstract quality. A great tool can still be wrong for the current team, architecture, or product stage. I try to evaluate technology through the reality of the actual work rather than the idealized use case from the demo.

That is especially important when the software supports operational teams. If a tool makes engineering happier but creates fragility for the product or delivery process, that tradeoff needs to be visible early.

Why I like writing these impressions down

Writing makes the evaluation sharper. It is easy to say something feels promising. It is harder and more useful to explain why it might matter, where it fits, and what would need to be true for it to earn adoption.

That is part of why I want this blog to include more technology impressions. The goal is not to react to everything. The goal is to build better judgment over time.

Built for clarity, speed, and a better first impression.
